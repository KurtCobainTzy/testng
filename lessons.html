Module 01

Learn How To Play Guitar

Welcome to Guitarify's Beginner Guitar Series! If you've just picked up a guitar for the first time — or if you've been meaning to learn for years — you're in exactly the right place. This module gives you the big picture of what learning guitar actually looks like, and how to set yourself up for success from day one.

Learning guitar is a journey — start at your own pace.

What Kind of Guitar Should You Start With?

Before anything else, you need a guitar to play. There are three main types beginners encounter: acoustic (steel string), classical (nylon string), and electric.

  • Acoustic (Steel String) — The most popular beginner choice. Loud, versatile, and no amp needed. Best for pop, folk, rock, and OPM songs.
  • Classical (Nylon String) — Easier on your fingers because the strings are softer. Wider neck though, which can be tricky. Great for school settings.
  • Electric — Requires an amplifier. Strings are thinner and easier to press, but you need extra equipment. Best for rock and pop styles.
💡 Beginner Tip

If you're unsure, start with a steel-string acoustic or a classical guitar. Both are affordable, require no extra equipment, and will teach you everything you need to know. Check the Recommendations page for specific guitar picks!

What Does the Learning Journey Look Like?

Learning guitar isn't about memorizing every note on the fretboard in week one. It's about building small, consistent habits. Here's a realistic roadmap:

1
Weeks 1–2: The Basics
Learn how to hold the guitar, tune it, and press your first chord shapes. Your fingers will hurt a little at first — that's completely normal. Calluses will form within 2–3 weeks.
2
Weeks 3–4: First Chords & Strumming
Learn 3–4 basic chords (Em, Am, G, D) and practice simple strumming patterns. By this point, you can play basic songs.
3
Month 2–3: Playing Real Songs
Combine chords and strumming to play full songs. Focus on smooth chord changes. This is where it gets really fun.
4
Month 4+: Building Your Skills
Expand your chord library, learn different strumming patterns, explore basic music theory, and start learning your favorite songs by ear.

How Long Should You Practice?

Short, consistent practice sessions are far more effective than occasional long ones. Aim for 15–30 minutes per day. That's it. Your fingers, brain, and muscle memory will thank you. Three 20-minute sessions per week will get you further than one 3-hour marathon on Sunday.

🎯 The Golden Rule

Practice slowly and correctly. It's better to play a chord perfectly at half speed than sloppily at full speed. Bad habits are very hard to unlearn once they're locked in. Take your time.

What Else Will You Need?

  • A clip-on tuner — Always tune before you play. An out-of-tune guitar sounds terrible no matter how well you play.
  • A guitar pick (plectrum) — Start with medium thickness. They're versatile and easy to control.
  • Patience — The first few weeks feel awkward. Your fingers will hurt. Chords will buzz. This is totally normal. Push through it.

This Is It — Let's Begin

Everything you need is right here on Guitarify. Go through the modules in order — each one builds on the last. By the end of this series, you'll be playing real songs. Let's go.

Module 02

How To Hold The Guitar

The way you hold your guitar affects everything — your comfort, how long you can play without pain, and how cleanly your chords ring out. Getting this right from day one will save you from developing bad habits that are hard to break later.

Sitting Position — The Casual Method

This is how most beginners start, and it works perfectly well. Sit in a chair without armrests so your arms can move freely.

1
Place the guitar on your right leg
If you're right-handed, rest the guitar's waist (the curved middle section) on your right thigh. Keep both feet flat on the floor.
2
Press the back of the guitar to your body
The back of the guitar body should lean against your stomach and chest. This keeps the guitar stable so you don't need to use your left hand to hold it up.
3
Keep the neck angled slightly upward
The neck of the guitar should point slightly upward, not down toward the floor. A downward neck forces your wrist into an awkward angle. Aim for about 30–45 degrees above horizontal.
4
Rest your strumming arm over the body
Let your right forearm rest naturally over the top of the guitar body. This anchors the guitar and keeps it from sliding. Your strumming hand should fall naturally near the sound hole.
5
Sit up straight
Keep your back straight but relaxed. Avoid slouching or hunching over the guitar to look at your fingers. Good posture prevents neck and back pain during long sessions.

Your Fretting Hand (Left Hand)

  • Thumb placement — Your thumb should rest on the back of the neck, roughly behind your middle finger.
  • Curved fingers — Curl your fingers so the tips press the strings, not the pads.
  • Press close to the fret — Place your fingertip just behind the metal fret for cleaner sound with less effort.
  • Fingers upright — Keep your fingertips as vertical as possible so they don't accidentally mute neighboring strings.
🚫 Common Mistake

Many beginners tilt the guitar body toward them so they can see the fretboard. This puts the neck in a bad angle and makes playing harder. Keep the guitar upright and face forward.

Your Fingers Will Hurt — That's Normal

The tips of your fretting fingers will feel sore after the first few practice sessions. Within 2–3 weeks of regular practice, your fingertips will toughen up and the pain disappears completely.

⚠️ Sharp Pain vs. Soreness

Fingertip soreness from strings is normal. However, sharp pain in your wrist, elbow, or shoulder is a sign of bad posture or technique. Never play through sharp pain.

Module 03

The Parts Of The Guitar

Before you can follow along with lessons, read chord diagrams, or communicate with other musicians, you need to know the names of the parts of your guitar. Don't worry — it's simpler than it looks!

The Three Main Sections

A
The Headstock (Top)
The flat piece at the very top of the neck. This is where the tuning pegs are located.
B
The Neck (Middle)
The long, thin section you hold with your fretting hand. Contains the fretboard where you press the strings.
C
The Body (Bottom)
The large, round part of the guitar. On an acoustic, this is where sound is produced through the hollow cavity and sound hole.

Key Parts — Neck Area

  • Fretboard (Fingerboard) — The flat front surface of the neck where you press strings.
  • Frets — Thin metal bars embedded across the fretboard. Each fret = one semitone interval.
  • Position Markers (Dots) — Visual landmarks at frets 3, 5, 7, 9, and 12.

Key Parts — Body Area

  • Sound Hole — Projects the guitar's sound on acoustic guitars.
  • Bridge — Where strings are anchored to the body.
  • Strap Buttons — Two metal pins where you attach your guitar strap.
💡 Quick Memory Trick

Remember it from top to bottom: Head → Neck → Body. Like a person!

Module 04

The Guitar String Names

Knowing the names of your six strings is one of the very first things you need to memorize as a guitarist. String names are used in chord diagrams, tuning, tabs, and when talking to other guitarists.

Standard Tuning — EADGBE

Going from the thickest string to the thinnest, the notes are:

E A D G B E
"Eddie Ate Dynamite, Good Bye Eddie"
The most popular way to remember the string order — from thickest to thinnest.
📝 Practice Exercise

Every time you pick up your guitar today, point to each string one by one and say its name out loud. Start at the thickest: "E... A... D... G... B... e." Do this before every session for a week and you'll have it memorized permanently.

Module 05

How To Tune Your Guitar

Always tune your guitar before you play. Always. An out-of-tune guitar sounds terrible no matter how well you play, and it also trains your ear to hear the wrong notes.

The Easiest Way: Clip-On Tuner

1
Clip the tuner onto the headstock
Clip it snugly with the screen facing you.
2
Turn it on and pluck one string
Pluck the 6th string (Low E) cleanly. The tuner will display what note it's detecting.
3
Read the needle
Needle LEFT = flat (tighten). Needle RIGHT = sharp (loosen). Center = in tune.
4
Repeat for all 6 strings
Go E → A → D → G → B → e. After tuning all six, go back and check them again.
💡 Pro Tip: Tune Up, Not Down

Always approach the target note from below (from flat). If you overshoot, loosen past the note, then tighten up to it again. The string stays in tune longer this way.

⚠️ App vs. Clip-On

Tuner apps work well in a quiet room but can struggle with background noise. A clip-on tuner reads vibrations directly from the guitar wood and works anywhere.

Module 06

How To Strum The Guitar

Strumming is how you bring chords to life and give music its rhythm. Good strumming technique creates a smooth, consistent sound — bad technique makes everything sound choppy and uneven.

Using a Pick (Plectrum)

  • Hold the pick between the tip of your index finger and thumb
  • The pick should stick out about 1cm from your fingers
  • Hold it firmly but not too tight — it needs to flex against the strings
  • Angle the pick slightly toward the headstock for smoother strumming

The Basic Down Strum

1
Position your strumming arm
Rest your forearm on the top edge of the guitar body. Your hand should hover just above the strings near the sound hole.
2
Strum in one smooth downward motion
Move the pick downward, brushing across all strings from thickest to thinnest. The motion comes from your wrist and forearm together.
3
Keep the motion loose and relaxed
Tension is the enemy of good tone. Relax your grip and let the pick glide across the strings.
↓ ↑ ↓ ↑
Down → Up → Down → Up
Count out loud: "1 and 2 and 3 and 4 and"
🎵 Practice Without Chords First

Before adding chord shapes, practice strumming patterns on an open guitar (no fretting). This lets you focus 100% on rhythm and pick technique.

Module 07

Your First Guitar Chords

This is the moment you've been building toward! You'll learn your first two guitar chords: Em (E minor) and Am (A minor). Both only require two to three fingers.

How to Read a Chord Diagram

  • Vertical lines = the 6 strings (left = Low E, right = High e)
  • Horizontal lines = the frets
  • Dots = where to place your fingers
  • O above a string = play that string open
  • X above a string = don't play that string (mute it)

Chord 1 — E Minor (Em)

Em
Finger 2 (middle) → 2nd fret, A string
Finger 3 (ring) → 2nd fret, D string
Strum all 6 strings ✓

Chord 2 — A Minor (Am)

Am
Finger 1 (index) → 1st fret, B string
Finger 2 (middle) → 2nd fret, D string
Finger 3 (ring) → 2nd fret, G string
Strum strings 5–1 only ✓
Em → Am → Em → Am
Strum 4x each chord. Count: 1-2-3-4 | 1-2-3-4
⚠️ Common Beginner Mistakes

Buzzing strings — Finger isn't pressing firmly enough or touching adjacent string. Muted strings — Finger laying flat and muting neighbors. Keep fingertips upright and press just behind the fret.

Module 08

Two More Guitar Chords

You've got Em and Am down! Now adding D major and G major. With these four chords together, you can play hundreds of real songs.

Chord 3 — D Major

D
Finger 1 (index) → 2nd fret, G string
Finger 2 (middle) → 2nd fret, High e string
Finger 3 (ring) → 3rd fret, B string
Strum strings 4–1 only ✓

Chord 4 — G Major

G
Finger 1 (index) → 2nd fret, A string
Finger 2 (middle) → 3rd fret, Low E string
Finger 4 (pinky) → 3rd fret, High e string
Strum all 6 strings ✓
G → D → Em → C
One of the most popular chord progressions in all of pop music
Module 09

Playing Your First Song

This is what it all leads to — playing an actual song! Using just the four chords you've learned (Em, Am, G, D), you can already play real music.

Choosing Your First Song

  • "Wonderwall" — Oasis — one of the most famous beginner songs ever
  • "Knocking on Heaven's Door" — Bob Dylan — just 3 chords, very slow and easy
  • "Leaving on a Jet Plane" — John Denver — slow and singable
  • Any OPM ballad you love — search "[song name] guitar chords" on Google

Step-by-Step: How to Learn a Song

1
Find the chord chart
Search "[song name] chords" on Google. Look for a "beginner" or "simplified" version that uses only basic open chords.
2
Learn each chord shape first
Make sure you can cleanly play each individual chord. For unfamiliar chords, check the Chord Library page.
3
Practice the chord changes
Practice only those specific transitions over and over until they feel automatic.
4
Add a simple strum pattern
Start with just one down strum per chord. Once that's smooth, add a basic down-down-up strum.
5
Play along with the recording
Put on the song and play along. Even if you miss some chord changes, keep strumming. Your timing will improve dramatically.
🎵 The 80% Rule

Don't wait until a song is perfect before moving to the next one. When you can play through it at about 80% accuracy without stopping, move on. Return to it later and you'll find it suddenly feels easy.

Module 10

Musical Strumming Tips

You've learned basic strumming — now let's make it sound musical. Great strumming isn't just about hitting the right pattern; it's about feel, dynamics, and rhythm.

Tip 1: Dynamics — Play Soft and Loud

  • Strum lightly and close to the sound hole for a soft, warm tone
  • Strum harder and toward the bridge for a brighter, louder tone
  • In verses, play softer. In choruses, play louder and more energetically

Tip 2: Muting for Rhythm Effects

✋ How to Palm Mute

Gently lay the edge of your palm on the strings right where they meet the bridge saddle. Strum while maintaining that light contact. Experiment with how much pressure for the right sound.

Tip 3: Rhythm — Feel the Beat

  • Count out loud — Say "1, 2, 3, 4" while you strum.
  • Use a metronome — Set it to 60 BPM and strum one chord per beat.
  • Tap your foot — Your foot becomes an internal metronome.

Tip 4: Don't Stop the Strumming Arm

Your strumming arm should keep moving even on beats where you're not hitting the strings. Think of it like a pendulum. Ghost strums keep your timing locked even during chord changes.

🎵 Practice Routine for Strumming

Day 1–2: Simple all-down strums. Day 3–4: Add up strums on the "and" of beats. Day 5–7: Try the full DDUUDU pattern slowly. Only speed up when it feels natural.

Learn C Major
